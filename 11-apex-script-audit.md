## The Core Problem Across All Scripts

Before line-by-line: every Apex script has the same structural failure. They describe the service. They don't enter the prospect's conversation. Great cold email doesn't explain what you do — it mirrors exactly what's keeping the prospect up at night and makes them feel like you already understand their situation before they've said a word.

## SCRIPT 1 AUDIT — AI Offer Angle 1

**Original:**
> Hey {{firstName}}, if someone in {{city}} is searching for {{custom line}} on ChatGPT does your clinic/law firm/?
> We bypass traditional search and place your clinic/firm directly inside ChatGPT. When patients/prospects/businesses ask AI for services you cover, we make it cite/recommend your business.
> Can I send more info?

**Verdict: 3/10**

**What's broken:**

1. **The opening question has no punch.** "If someone is searching for X does your clinic show up?" — they don't know the answer, and "if" removes all urgency. There's no consequence built into the question.

2. **The template variables are doing no work.** `{{custom line}}` is a placeholder in the copy itself — if this is what's going out, the personalization is fake. Prospects can smell this.

3. **"clinic/law firm/?"** — the slash and trailing question mark is a formatting accident that never got cleaned up. This alone signals low effort and kills trust instantly.

4. **"We bypass traditional search"** — generic mechanism claim. Every SEO and AI tool says this. It means nothing.

5. **"place your clinic/firm directly inside ChatGPT"** — this is the core claim and it's completely unexplained. How? What does that look like? The skepticism this triggers is never addressed.

6. **"cite/recommend"** — pick one word. Slashes signal copy that wasn't finished.

7. **"Can I send more info?"** — the lowest-conversion CTA in cold email. You're asking them to say yes to receiving a pitch. No specificity on what they'd be getting.

**Rewritten — Angle 1:**

Subject: ChatGPT is sending {{city}} patients to your competitors

Hey {{firstName}},

I just searched "{{specific search term, e.g. best orthodontist in Dubai}}" on ChatGPT.

It didn't mention {{Company Name}}.

It recommended {{Competitor 1}} and {{Competitor 2}}.

That's a problem — because in the next 12 months, AI is going to handle more patient searches than Google. The practices showing up there are building a lead pipeline that SEO can't touch.

We fix this. We've done it for {{similar clinic in same specialty}}. They went from zero AI citations to showing up in 4 of the top 5 AI assistants within 60 days.

Worth a 15-minute call to show you what that looks like for {{Company Name}}?

{{signature}}

---

**Why this converts better:**
- Specific action taken by the sender (searched, found a result) — creates credibility
- Competitor named = immediate FOMO trigger
- Stakes established (AI handling more searches than Google)
- Proof point with timeline (60 days)
- CTA is specific (15 minutes, you'll see what it looks like for THEM)

## SCRIPT 2 AUDIT — AI Offer Angle 2

**Original:**
> Hey {{FirstName}}, I was searching for {{custom line}} on ChatGPT and it recommended me your competitor {{CompetitorName}} instead of you.
> We can actually place your clinic/firm directly inside ChatGPT so when patients/prospects/businesses ask AI for services you cover, we make it cite/recommend your business.
> Can I send more info?

**Verdict: 5/10** — Strongest of the three AI angles. The competitor hook is powerful. It's being buried by weak execution.

**What's broken:**

1. **"recommended me your competitor"** — grammatically awkward. Should be "recommended your competitor."

2. **"We can actually place"** — "actually" is a hedge. It implies you're surprised it works. Remove it.

3. **"clinic/firm"** — still the slash problem. Pick the industry before you send.

4. **"when patients/prospects/businesses ask AI"** — who are we talking to? Pick one. This ICP confusion weakens trust.

5. **"cite/recommend"** — still the slash issue.

6. **"Can I send more info?"** — still weak. What info? Why would they care?

**Rewritten — Angle 2:**

Subject: ChatGPT recommended {{CompetitorName}} when I searched for you

Hey {{firstName}},

I searched "{{specific service}} in {{city}}" on ChatGPT this morning.

It recommended {{CompetitorName}}.

Not {{Company Name}}.

We engineer content that forces AI assistants — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini — to recommend your practice instead. Not ads. Not SEO. The actual AI response changes.

We've done this for {{similar business}}. They now show up in 6 out of 10 AI searches for their category in {{city}}.

Can I send you a 90-second screen recording showing exactly what this looks like?

{{signature}}

---

**Why this converts better:**
- Subject line is a pattern interrupt — they want to know what you found
- "this morning" adds recency and credibility
- Short punchy sentences. White space. Easy to read in 10 seconds.
- "Not {{Company Name}}" — lands the pain before explaining the solution
- Specific mechanism (content engineering, not vague "placement")
- Proof point is specific (6 out of 10 searches)
- CTA offers a 90-second video — low friction, high curiosity

## SCRIPT 3 AUDIT — SaaS Angle

**Original:**
> Hey {{firstName}}, when a business asks ChatGPT for the best {{software category}}, does your platform actually show up?
> We bypass traditional SEO and place your software directly inside AI assistants. When teams ask AI for solutions in your space, we engineer the content so it automatically cites and recommends your product.
> Can I send over a quick breakdown of how we do this?

**Verdict: 6/10** — Best of the three AI angles structurally. The opening question is sharp. Still has execution problems.

**What's broken:**

1. **"does your platform actually show up?"** — good question, but "actually" is doing unnecessary work. "Does your platform show up?" is cleaner.

2. **"We bypass traditional SEO"** — same vague claim. "Bypass" implies illegitimacy. Reframe as "We engineer content specifically for how AI reads and ranks information."

3. **"place your software directly inside AI assistants"** — same mechanism vagueness as before.

4. **"automatically cites and recommends"** — the word "automatically" implies it's easy and perhaps not durable. The reality is it requires active content engineering. Lean into that — it's a moat.

5. **"Can I send over a quick breakdown?"** — "quick breakdown" undervalues the offer. Call it what it is.

**Rewritten — SaaS Angle:**

Subject: {{CompetitorName}} shows up on ChatGPT. {{Company Name}} doesn't.

Hey {{firstName}},

Searched "best {{software category}} for {{use case}}" on ChatGPT.

{{CompetitorName}} came up first. {{Company Name}} wasn't mentioned.

Here's why it matters: B2B buyers now start software research on AI before they hit Google. The platforms showing up there are winning the evaluation before it starts.

We engineer the content architecture that makes AI assistants recommend your product when buyers search your category. {{Similar SaaS company}} went from zero AI citations to being the #1 recommended tool in their category on ChatGPT and Perplexity in 45 days.

Want me to send a 2-minute breakdown of what that looks like for {{Company Name}}?

{{signature}}

## SCRIPT 4 AUDIT — Offer 2 (CRM/Ads Angle)

**Original:**
> Hey {{firstName}}, how much payroll are you wasting on agents chasing dead leads because your current ads optimize for cheap form fills?
> We feed your CRM deal-stage data directly back into Meta and Google, forcing the algorithm to stop buying junk leads and strictly target people who actually close.
> For one real estate investment firm, we implemented this exact structure and scaled them from 3 to 17 qualified buyer leads per day.
> Open to a free Ad Account Audit showing exactly how much of your current budget is wasted on leads that cannot close?

**Verdict: 7.5/10** — The strongest script in the set by a significant margin. The mechanism is specific, the proof point is concrete, and the CTA offers clear value. The problems are refinement-level.

**What's broken:**

1. **"how much payroll are you wasting"** — excellent opener but slightly aggressive. It assumes they know they have this problem. If they've never heard of CRM signal feedback loops, this framing might confuse before it compels. Solution: add one more beat of specificity to confirm they're feeling this pain.

2. **"forcing the algorithm"** — "forcing" is good. Strong verb. Keep it.

3. **"junk leads"** — informal but works for real estate. Keep if the audience is real estate. Replace with "unqualified leads" for more formal verticals.

4. **"leads that cannot close"** — should be "can't close." "Cannot" is stiff.

5. **"For one real estate investment firm"** — the proof point needs a timeframe. 3 to 17 leads in HOW LONG? Without a timeframe it sounds unverifiable.

6. **"Open to a free Ad Account Audit"** — "Open to" is passive. "Want" or "Interested in" converts better.

**Rewritten — Offer 2:**

Subject: Your Meta ads are buying leads your sales team can't close

Hey {{firstName}},

Your ads are optimizing for the wrong thing.

Meta and Google don't know which leads close — they only know who filled out a form. So they find you more form-fillers. Your agents spend 80% of their time on people who never had the intent to buy.

We fix this by feeding your CRM deal-stage data directly back into the ad platform. The algorithm stops buying form-fillers and starts buying buyers.

One real estate investment firm went from 3 to 17 qualified buyer leads per day — in 34 days — without touching their ad budget.

Worth a free Ad Account Audit? I'll identify exactly how much of your current spend is going to leads that can't close, and show you what fixing that looks like on your specific account.

{{signature}}

---

**Why this converts better:**
- "Your ads are optimizing for the wrong thing" — this is a pattern interrupt that doesn't require them to know the technical problem
- The explanation is simpler and more visual ("they find you more form-fillers")
- "34 days" — added timeframe. Now it's verifiable and specific
- "without touching their ad budget" — this removes the cost objection before it's raised
- CTA is active ("Worth a free audit?") and hyper-specific about what they'll receive

## Universal Copy Rules for All ALIF Scripts

**1. One ICP per email.** Not "clinic/law firm/SaaS." Pick one. The more specific, the higher the reply rate.

**2. Name the competitor.** If you can research it, name it. "Your competitor" is weak. "DrSmith Dental" is powerful.

**3. No slashes in copy.** Ever. Slashes signal an unfinished template.

**4. Proof points need three elements:** Who, What Result, How Long. "A real estate firm got 17 leads" is weak. "A Dubai-based investment firm went from 3 to 17 qualified buyer leads in 34 days" is a proof point.

**5. The CTA should offer something specific.** Not "more info." A 90-second video. A free audit. A specific finding. Something they can picture receiving.

**6. Subject lines should make them feel something in 3 seconds.** FOMO, curiosity, or mild threat to their competitive position.

**7. Short sentences. White space. Max 5 lines of body copy.** If they have to scroll to read it, you've already lost.
